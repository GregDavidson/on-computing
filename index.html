Simple Notes on Computing

If you want information about science, mathematics and technology, there is a great deal of it on the web, much of it of excellent quality and great detail, in some cases providing comprehensive, encyclopedic completeness. However, this sheer mass of information can obscure the fundamentals and an understanding of the fundamentals is required to make the whole mass of information comprehensible and useful. In other words, a great amount of information can be a serious barrier to acquiring knowledge.

What to take as fundamental requires a selection among multiple options. This is especially true of computing which can be constructed from the simplicity of switches, the elegance of recursive function theory or a number of other excellent approaches. Ideally a student or practitioner would explore more than one construction of their field of study. These Simple Notes On Computing are the beginnings of an attempt on my part to provide a foundation for the art and science of computing which is accessible to any curious individual. I stay close to the physical and avoid advanced mathematics.

I wish to emphasise that even if I should develop these notes to some reasonable state of completeness, they will not be sufficient as a foundation for any degree of mastery of computing. A student of computing who finds this material intriguing must follow it up with significant practical work along with more abstract theoretical studies. I intend to add to this work some material along those lines which I have written and some links to some of the best work to be found elsewhere.

What Computers Are

This Simple Note is intended to cover the very basics of What Computers Are for someone who thinks they might want to go on and learn a lot about computers and computing. The intended reader is someone with the patience for a very careful, but not difficult, introduction.

A Model Computer

This Simple Note will introduce a simple yet powerful computer which we can explore in order to better understand What Computers Are and how we can build powerful systems with them. You should read the earlier Simple Note: What Computers Are first.

It will become clear that there are at least three distinct computational models of increasing abstraction and power, each built on the level below it

  1. Building procedures which apply operations to data.
  2. Declaring relationships which define data structures and algorithms.
  3. Distinguishing and characterizing domain spaces and search strategies.

It will also become clear that working with computers can be done in very different ways:

  1. Constructing desired programs by writing code in (hopefully high level) languages.
  2. Writing declarative and meta code to indirectly construct programs.
  3. Partnering with expert systems to go beyond normal human and machine capacities.
Advanced Representation

The power of our computers is primarily a function of our strategies for representing information and knowledge. Advanced representations are build on top of the simple bits, bytes and words introduced in the earlier Simple Note: What Computers Are which you should read first.

When complete, this Simple Note will introduce and illustrate some of the most powerful strategies currently known for representing informaition and knowledge, including:

  • relations
  • graph structures
  • objects
  • frames
  • schemas
  • predicate calculus
  • expert systems
